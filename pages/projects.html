Professional Development Supports for Teaching Bioinformatics through Mobile Learning (K-12 Bioinformatics)
NSF Discovery Research K-12 · 2018–2024 · Urban air quality & bioinformatics · Mobile learning
Current NSF DRK-12

The major goal of this project is to co-construct and field-test multidisciplinary high school lessons organized around bioinformatics concepts through existing educational research. The lessons engage students in real-world data collection to investigate air quality issues and their relationship with asthma by using a mobile phone and sensors to collect local data in students' neighborhoods (e.g., particulate matter 2.5 and carbon monoxide).

These lessons are anchored in culturally meaningful learning, real-world inquiry, data literacy, contextualization with mobile technology, and cognitively-rich pedagogies through problem-based learning (PBL).

Over the course of this project, we have:

(a) designed an entirely novel bioinformatics curriculum for high school students; (b) partnered with teachers to enact that curriculum with hundreds of high school students from a diverse set of partner schools; (c) run a variety of in-person and online workshops with 15 high school science teachers in the greater Philadelphia area; (d) constructed and delivered a fully online professional development (PD) experience using edX; and (e) designed new methods for teacher training that promote teacher pedagogical content knowledge for data literacy and resource activation.

↗ NSF Award #1812738
Related Research Lines

Epistemic Emotions & Practices

PM: Jooeun Shim · js4719@tc.columbia.edu

Investigates how students' epistemic emotions — curiosity, confusion, surprise — shape engagement in data inquiry and analysis within K–12 bioinformatics contexts. The project examines how emotional responses arise during data investigation and how they influence students' epistemic practices.

↗ ICLS 2025 Paper

Student Artifact Analysis

PM: Jooeun Shim · js4719@tc.columbia.edu

Investigates how students' learning in K–12 bioinformatics extends beyond understanding to informed action. Grounded in Hodson's framework of science education for action, the study examines how students use data-driven insights to identify issues, make decisions, and take action on real-world problems.

Bichronous Learning

PM: Sue Kwon · suekwon@upenn.edu

Examines a bichronous learning model that purposefully integrates synchronous and asynchronous modalities to deliver a flexible, interactive online professional development program for high school science teachers. Participants engage in real-time discussions while completing self-paced tasks on their own schedule.

DeTECT: Developing Teachers' Epistemic Cognition and Teaching Practices for Supporting Students' Epistemic Practices with Scientific Systems
NSF Discovery Research K-12 · 2020–2026 · Epistemic practices · Complex systems · Global partnerships
Current NSF DRK-12

The ultimate goal of DeTECT is to support the development of students' epistemic practices and cognition while promoting their interest and skills in investigating and evaluating scientific phenomena. To accomplish this goal, we are constructing, delivering, and pilot testing a high-quality teacher professional development program using complex systems curricula and agent-based simulations.

Over the course of this project, we have:

(a) run a variety of in-person and online workshops with 12 expert partner teachers from the USA, India, and Kenya; (b) designed novel PD resources to support the integration of opportunities for robust and authentic epistemic practices inside real-world classrooms; (c) developed a new complex systems curriculum and agent-based simulation on StarLogo Nova to engage students in a realistic pandemic investigation; (d) adapted an existing complex systems curriculum (BioGraph) to promote additional opportunities for students to engage in meaningful epistemic practices and to more directly incorporate socioscientific issues (SSIs); and (e) supported the implementation of these new curricular resources with hundreds of students across the globe.

↗ NSF Award #2009803
DeTECT Research Lines

Instructional Epistemic Frames

Examines teacher moves intended to support students' authentic engagement with and understanding of scientific practices in professional development settings of high school biology teachers.

↗ ICLS 2024 Paper

Epistemic Disagreements

PM: Noora Noushad · noora@gse.upenn.edu

Explores mechanisms that help teachers navigate an increasingly complex epistemic space by bringing elements of epistemic conflict into classrooms productively. Three lines of inquiry: redesigning science learning, developing teacher mediation moves, and epistemic performance in algorithmically mediated environments.

Epistemic Vigilance

PM: Claire LeBovidge · cleb@upenn.edu

Designed to help students navigate scientific misinformation encountered in everyday life. Students examine four sources about energy drinks and mental health, developing shared criteria of credible source characteristics. Introduces key concepts such as review studies, confirmation bias, and cherry-picking.

Collaborative Design

PM: Claire LeBovidge · cleb@upenn.edu

Collaborative design (co-design) is marked by shared agency among teachers and researchers in shaping both the design process and its products. Teachers participate in iterative cycles of design, classroom enactment, and revision — some partnerships spanning over a decade.

Past Projects
BioGraph 2.0 – Professional Development for High School Biology Teachers for Teaching and Learning About Complex Systems
NSF DRK-12, 2017–2021 · Online PD on edX · Global reach
PastNSF DRK-12

Our team designed a large-scale fully online PD program to improve access to BioGraph resources and high-quality pedagogical practices at relatively low cost. This program was hosted on edX and was accessed by hundreds of teachers from across the globe. As a result of this program, the BioGraph curriculum was implemented with thousands of students in a wide range of diverse educational contexts.

As with the original BioGraph program, the simulations and curricular resources can be found on the BioGraph Moodle site.

↗ NSF Award #1721003
BioGraph – Graphical Programming for Constructing Complex Systems Understanding in Biology
NSF DRK-12, 2010–2015 · Agent-based simulations · High school biology
PastNSF DRK-12

Our team developed agent-based simulations and a five-lesson high school biology curriculum to support the teaching of complex systems ideas and concepts in high school science classrooms. The simulations and curriculum were developed in strong partnership with high school science teachers, many of whom still collaborate with CRADLE Lab to this day.

The simulations and curricular resources can be found on the BioGraph Moodle site. These simulations and curricular resources served as a foundational building block for the DeTECT project.

↗ NSF Award #1019228
App Inventor for Socio-scientific Issues to Build Engagement in STEM
University Research Fund & Milken Foundation, 2016–2017 · Middle school app design
Past

This project supported middle school students as they developed their own app to address or raise awareness about locally-relevant socioscientific issues of their choosing. Over the course of this program, students learned to use the App Inventor programming language before utilizing an iterative process to design, adapt, and revise student-generated apps to challenge the issues that students found most personally relevant.

ARIEL — Augmented Reality for Interpretive and Experiential Learning
NSF AISL, 2008–2014 · Franklin Institute Science Museum · Middle school
PastNSF AISL

This project integrated augmented reality (AR) learning experiences into an exhibit at the Franklin Institute Science Museum. These experiences were targeted primarily towards middle school-aged students and covered topics such as aerodynamics and physics.

↗ NSF Award #0741659
Nanotechnology and Bioengineering in Philadelphia Public Schools
NSF ITEST, 2008–2012 · 18 Philadelphia-area schools · Thousands of students
PastNSF ITEST

This project involved partnering with teachers and guidance counselors from 18 schools across the greater Philadelphia area to increase student knowledge of nanotechnology and bioengineering, enhance student skills at using information and educational technologies, and increase student awareness of post-secondary educational opportunities and careers in science, technology, and engineering.

Through a series of workshops, online communications and support, field trips, and a community expo, thousands of high school students were impacted by this project.
